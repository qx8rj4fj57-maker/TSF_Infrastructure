Infrastructure Build
THE FIVE PILLARS
The 10 rubric criteria collapse into five operational build priorities. Each has concrete deliverables and a 2026 target.
  • Pillar 1 — Legal Entity & Governance. Board of directors, bylaws, formal entity, succession policy. The mechanism that makes the program an institution.
  • Pillar 2 — Administrative Infrastructure. Program Director separate from coaching. Operational systems for travel, contracts, budgets, communications.
  • Pillar 3 — Mission, Brand Identity & Documentation. Formalized mission. Complete evidence package: ranked players, D1 alumni, results history, program narrative.
  • Pillar 4 — Market Position & Regional Network. Territory thesis with competitive landscape. Multi-state relationships documented with endorsement letters.
  • Pillar 5 — Age Group Depth & Youth Pipeline. Competitive rosters at 15U, 16U, 17U. Middle school / junior program launched with structured curriculum.
Why Pillars 1 & 2 Come First
The head coach cannot be the contract manager, travel coordinator, financial officer, and communications director. That model has a hard ceiling — and it is a significant red flag for any evaluator. Governance and admin are the structural preconditions for everything else.
The Documentation Imperative
Pillar 3 is often overlooked by established programs. The evidence exists — ranked players, D1 alumni, coaching dossiers, event results, program history — but it is not compiled. Unverifiable is functionally the same as nonexistent when evaluators are making decisions.

Circuit Pathways
THREE ROUTES TO THE TOP
Nike EYBL is the highest-visibility grassroots circuit. There are three credible routes to it. The infrastructure build is a prerequisite for all three.
A — Direct to EYBL
Full infrastructure + EYBL-level 17U talent + established visibility at Nike-affiliated events + open market territory. All variables must converge simultaneously. The fastest route — and the hardest to control.
WINDOW: Late 2026 application → 2027–28 EYBL
  • All 5 infrastructure pillars complete
  • Ranked players, multi-age competitive depth
  • Visibility at Nike EYBL / Scholastic or Nike-adjacent events
  • Market territory with no Nike conflict
B — Secondary Circuit → EYBL
Infrastructure + sponsored status with a legitimate secondary brand circuit (UAA, 3SSB, Puma CYB, NB Nationals) + 1–2 seasons of documented performance. Can be the most viable route for programs that eventually reach EYBL. Builds the competitive record that significantly strengthens any subsequent EYBL conversation.
WINDOW: 2027 secondary → 2028–29 EYBL conversation
  • Pillars 1–4 complete before brand commitment
  • 16U + 17U competitive with verifiable results
  • Fit with target brand's circuit geography
  • 1–2 seasons proving-ground willingness
C — Competitor Top Tier → EYBL
Full infrastructure + top-tier Adidas / Under Armour / Puma / NB status + sustained multi-season performance + available at moment of Nike market opening. Longest path. Historically documented from research. Most durable if the competitor relationship is the right fit.
WINDOW: 2027–28 top tier → 2028–29+ EYBL
  • Full infrastructure before any brand commitment
  • Achieve and sustain top tier — 2+ seasons
  • Territory remains a Nike opportunity
  • Maintain Nike community relationships independently
Pathway Analysis
ROUTE COMPARISON
All three paths share one prerequisite: the infrastructure described in this plan.
WHAT MAKES PATH A VIABLE
The direct route does not happen in a meeting room. Decision makers at Nike know — through their own regional staff, through the coaching community, through event attendance — which programs are operating at the required level. Being known before the conversation is as important as any documentation. If the program is not already visible at Nike-adjacent events, Path A is speculative regardless of the application quality.
The Relationship Reality
Relationships with brands exist at the program level already. The infrastructure formalizes the organization around those relationships — so when a conversation gets serious, there is a real entity, a real board, real documentation, and a real operational team behind it.
WHY PATH B & C MATTER
Secondary and competitor circuits are not consolation prizes. They are the proving ground where programs build the operational experience, competitive record, and institutional credibility that makes a top-tier conversation viable. Programs that have been operating at the highest level of a secondary circuit for two seasons enter the EYBL conversation with documentation that programs seeking direct entry cannot produce.
  • Secondary circuits develop operational muscle — multi-event, multi-age, multi-state logistics
  • Competitor top-tier programs have documented performance that transfers across brands
  • Both paths produce the kind of proven track record that reduces evaluation risk for decision makers
  • A program that builds its infrastructure first is in position for any of the three paths simultaneously
